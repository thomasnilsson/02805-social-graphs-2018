'''Suzanne Marie Bonamici''' (born October 14, 1954) is an American politician from [[Oregon]]. A member of the [[Democratic Party (United States)|Democratic Party]], she is the U.S. Representative for [[Oregon's 1st congressional district|the state's 1st congressional district]] and was first elected in a [[Oregon's 1st congressional district special election, 2012|special election]] on January 31, 2012. The district includes most of [[Portland, Oregon|Portland]] west of the [[Willamette River]], as well as all of [[Yamhill County, Oregon|Yamhill]], [[Columbia County, Oregon|Columbia]], [[Clatsop County, Oregon|Clatsop]], and [[Washington County, Oregon|Washington]] counties.

Bonamici previously represented District 17 in the [[Oregon State Senate]] from 2008 to 2011.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://blog.oregonlive.com/mapesonpolitics/2011/11/suzanne_bonamici_makes_legisla.html|work=[[The Oregonian]]|title=Suzanne Bonamici makes legislative resignation official|date=November 21, 2011}}</ref> She was first elected to the [[Oregon House of Representatives]] in 2006.

==Early life, education, and law career==
Bonamici was born in [[Detroit]] and raised in a small Michigan town. Her father worked for a local bank and her mother was a small-business owner and piano teacher. She earned an A.A. from [[Lane Community College]], a B.A. in journalism from the [[University of Oregon]], and a J.D. from the [[University of Oregon School of Law]].{{citation needed|date=November 2016}}

During college, she was a legal assistant at [[Lane County, Oregon|Lane County]] [[Legal aid in the United States|Legal Aid]] in the city of [[Eugene, Oregon|Eugene]]. After getting her law degree, she became a [[Consumer protection laws|consumer protection attorney]] for the [[Federal Trade Commission]] in [[Washington, D.C.|the nation's capital]]. She went into private practice in [[Portland, Oregon|Portland]] and represented small businesses.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.bonamiciforcongress.com/about |title=About Suzanne &#124; Suzanne Bonamici for Congress |website=Bonamiciforcongress.com |date=2012-01-31 |accessdate=2016-11-11}}</ref>

==Oregon legislature==

===Elections===
In 2006, incumbent Democratic State Representative [[Brad Avakian]] decided to retire to run for the [[Oregon Senate]]. She ran for the open seat in Oregon's 34th House District and defeated Republican Joan Draper 62%-36%.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=307673 |title=OR State House 34 Race - Nov 07, 2006 |publisher=Our Campaigns |date= |accessdate=2016-11-11}}</ref>

On April 30, 2008 Bonamici was appointed by Commissioners from both [[Washington County, Oregon|Washington]] and [[Multnomah County, Oregon|Multnomah]] counties to represent Oregon's 17th Senate District. The seat became vacant when Avakian was appointed Commissioner of the [[Oregon Bureau of Labor and Industries]].<ref name=senate>{{cite news
|title=Bonamici named to replace Avakian in state Senate
|date=May 1, 2008
|url=http://www.beavertonvalleytimes.com/news/story.php?story_id=120958893605414700S
|work=Beaverton Valley Times
}}</ref> She was sworn in on May 19, 2008.

Bonamici was unopposed [[Oregon legislative elections, 2008|in the November 2008 special election]] for the balance of Avakian's four-year term, and was elected with 97 percent of the vote.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=504691 |title=OR State Senate 17 - Special Election Race - Nov 04, 2008 |publisher=Our Campaigns |date= |accessdate=2016-11-11}}</ref> In 2010, she won re-election with 64% of the vote.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=681595 |title=OR State Senate 17 Race - Nov 02, 2010 |publisher=Our Campaigns |date= |accessdate=2016-11-11}}</ref>

===Committee assignments===
In the 2009 Legislative Session, Bonamici served as the Chair of the Senate Consumer Protection and Public Affairs Committee and as a member of the Senate Judiciary and Education Committees.{{citation needed|date=November 2016}}

==U.S. House of Representatives==

===Elections===
;Special election
{{Main|Oregon's 1st congressional district special election, 2012}} 
In early 2011, Bonamici's name was floated as a possible successor to then-Congressman [[David Wu]] after ''[[The Oregonian]]'' and ''[[Willamette Week]]'' reported Wu exhibited odd behavior and clashed with his staff in the midst of apparent mental illness during the 2010 election cycle.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.oregonlive.com/politics/index.ssf/2011/02/oregon_democratic_leaders_say.html|title=Oregon Democratic leaders say they hope Wu gets help, put off talk of his political future|date=February 19, 2011|accessdate=March 2, 2011|agency=OregonLive|work=The Oregonian}}</ref> Following Wu's resignation from Congress, Bonamici announced her candidacy for the [[Oregon's 1st congressional district special election, 2012|special election]] to replace him,<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.statesmanjournal.com/article/20110804/UPDATE/110804019/Bonamici-announces-bid-Wu-s-seat-Congress|work=[[Statesman Journal]]|accessdate=August 6, 2011|title=Bonamici announces bid for Wu's seat in Congress}}</ref> touting endorsements from former Governor [[Barbara Roberts]], former Congresswoman [[Elizabeth Furse]], and incumbent Oregon Attorney General [[John Kroger]], among others.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.bonamiciforcongress.com/endorsements|publisher=Bonamici for Congress|title=Endorsements|accessdate=August 6, 2011}}</ref>

On November 8, 2011, Bonamici won the [[Democratic Party of Oregon]]'s nomination, winning an outright majority of the vote in every county in the district and capturing 66% of the vote overall, with a 44-point margin over second-place finisher [[Brad Avakian]]. She faced [[Oregon Republican Party|Republican]] nominee Rob Cornilles in the special election on January 31, 2012,<ref>{{cite news|url=http://hosted.ap.org/dynamic/files/elections/2011/by_county/OR_Page_1108.html?SITE=AP&SECTION=POLITICS|work=Associated Press|title=Oregon - County Vote Results|date=November 9, 2011|accessdate=November 9, 2011}}</ref> winning by a 14-point margin.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://oregonvotes.org/results/2012S/868334206.html|title=Representative in Congress, 1st District Unofficial Results|publisher=Oregon Secretary of State|accessdate=February 1, 2012}}</ref>

Prior to her election to Congress, Bonamici resigned from the Oregon Senate on November 21,<ref>{{cite news|author=Staff|title=Physician to sit in state seat|url=http://www.oregonlive.com/argus/index.ssf/2011/12/physician_to_sit_in_state_seat.html|accessdate=December 22, 2011|newspaper=[[The Hillsboro Argus]]|date=December 22, 2011}}</ref> and was replaced by [[Elizabeth Steiner Hayward]] in December.<ref>{{cite news|last=Mapes|first=Jeff|title=A Washington County commissioner at sea casts decisive vote to fill Oregon Senate seat|url=http://www.oregonlive.com/politics/index.ssf/2011/12/a_washington_county_commission.html|accessdate=December 22, 2011|newspaper=The Oregonian|date=December 21, 2011}}</ref>

In November 2012, Bonamici won re-election to her first full term with over 60% of the vote.<ref>{{cite web|last=Brown|first=Kate|title=2012 election results|url=http://oregonvotes.org/doc/history/nov62012/G12_Abstract.pdf|publisher=OR STATE SEC OF STATE|accessdate=November 11, 2016}}</ref>

===Legislation===
On July 31, 2014, Bonamici introduced the [[Tsunami Warning, Education, and Research Act of 2014 (H.R. 5309; 113th Congress)]] into the House.<ref name=5309allactions>{{cite web|title=H.R. 5309 - All Actions|url=https://beta.congress.gov/bill/113th-congress/house-bill/5309/all-actions|publisher=United States Congress|accessdate=September 10, 2014}}</ref> The bill would authorize the [[National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration]] (NOAA) to spend $27 million a year for three years on their on-going [[tsunami]] warning and research programs.<ref name=MarcosHousePasses>{{cite news|last1=Marcos|first1=Cristina|title=House passes bill to authorize tsunami forecasting programs|url=http://thehill.com/blogs/floor-action/house/216987-house-passes-bill-to-authorize-tsunami-forecasting-programs|accessdate=September 10, 2014|publisher=The Hill|date=September 8, 2014}}</ref>

Bonamici said "the coastlines of the United States already play an integral role in the economic prosperity of this country and we must strengthen their preparedness and resiliency so they can continue to play that role going forward."<ref name="MarcosHousePasses"/> She added that this bill "will improve the country's understanding of the threat posed by tsunami events" because it will "improve forecasting and notification systems, support local community outreach and preparedness and response plans, and develop supportive technologies."<ref name=SaferCoastlines1>{{cite web|title=Representative Bonamici Introduces Bipartisan Tsunami Warning Bill|url=http://safercoastlines.com/2014/07/representative-bonamici-introduces-bipartisan-tsunami-warning-bill|publisher=Safer Coastlines|accessdate=September 10, 2014}}</ref>

==Personal life==
She is married to [[Michael H. Simon]], a federal judge.<ref>{{cite news|last=Duin|first=Steve|title=In the wake of David Wu case, accusations fly in Oregon's First District|url=http://www.oregonlive.com/news/oregonian/steve_duin/index.ssf/2011/08/in_the_wake_of_david_wu_case_a.html|accessdate=August 9, 2011|newspaper=The Oregonian|date=August 6, 2011}}</ref> They have two children, Andrew and Sara. Bonamici was raised Episcopalian and Unitarian, and now attends synagogue with her husband [[Michael H. Simon|Michael Simon]], who is Jewish, and their children.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://gov.oregonlive.com/election/2011/Congress/candidate/bonamici|title=Suzanne Bonamici|series=Oregon Special Election voter guide|work=The Oregonian|accessdate=January 23, 2012}}</ref>
