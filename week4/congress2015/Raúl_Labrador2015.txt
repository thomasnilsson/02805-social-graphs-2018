'''Raúl Rafael Labrador''' (born December 8, 1967) is an [[United States|American]] politician who has been the [[United States House of Representatives|U.S. Representative]] for {{ushr|ID|1}} since 2011. He is a member of the [[Republican Party (United States)|Republican Party]]. <!--His district is located in the northern and western portions of the state and includes the southern fourth of [[Boise, Idaho|Boise]] as well as the cities of [[Meridian, Idaho|Meridian]], [[Coeur d'Alene, Idaho|Coeur d'Alene]], [[Moscow, Idaho|Moscow]], [[Sandpoint, Idaho|Sandpoint]], [[Lewiston, Idaho|Lewiston]],  [[Bonners Ferry, Idaho|Bonners Ferry]], [[McCall, Idaho|McCall]], [[Caldwell, Idaho|Caldwell]], [[Nampa, Idaho|Nampa]], [[Emmett, Idaho|Emmett]], [[Parma, Idaho|Parma]], [[Weiser, Idaho|Weiser]], and [[Eagle, Idaho|Eagle]].--> Labrador previously represented District 14B in the [[Idaho House of Representatives]].

==Early life, education, and law career==
Born in [[Carolina, Puerto Rico]], Labrador relocated to [[Las Vegas]], [[Nevada]], as a child and graduated from [[Las Vegas High School]] in 1985. He attended [[Brigham Young University]] in [[Provo, Utah]], and spent two years as a [[Missionary (LDS Church)|missionary]] in [[Chile]], from 1987 to 1989. Labrador returned to BYU and received a [[Bachelor of Arts|B.A.]] in 1992, in [[Spanish language|Spanish]] with an emphasis in Latin American literature. He was admitted to the [[University of Washington]] [[University of Washington School of Law|School of Law]] in [[Seattle]] and received his [[Juris Doctor|J.D.]] in 1995. {{cn|date=November 2014}}

Married in 1991, Labrador relocated to his wife's home state of Idaho and practiced law and [[immigration law]] in private practice from 1995 until his election to the [[Idaho House of Representatives]] in 2006.<ref>{{cite web|title=Congressional Biographical Directory of the United States 1774–present|url=http://bioguide.congress.gov/scripts/biodisplay.pl?index=L000573}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|last=PARKER|first=ASHLEY and JULIA PRESTON|title=In House, Immigration Spurs Push by G.O.P|url=http://www.nytimes.com/2013/06/07/us/politics/house-republicans-seek-tougher-line-on-immigration.html?src=un&feedurl=http%3A%2F%2Fjson8.nytimes.com%2Fpages%2Fpolitics%2Findex.jsonp|accessdate=June 7, 2013|newspaper=NYT|date=June 6, 2013}}</ref>

==Idaho legislature==
Labrador was elected to represent the 14th district in the Idaho House of Representatives in 2006. While in the state legislature, he was a leader in the successful fight against the attempt to increase the state gasoline tax. {{cn|date=November 2014}}

==U.S. House of Representatives==
===Elections===
;2010
{{See also|United States House of Representatives elections in Idaho, 2010#District 1}}
In 2010, Labrador defeated [[Vaughn Ward]] in the Republican primary 48%-39% on May 10, in what was widely considered a major upset.<ref>http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=512944</ref><ref>[http://ktvb.zidaho.com/elections/house1.php U.S. House Dist. 1 GOP] KTVB.com Accessed June 1, 2010</ref><ref>{{cite web|first=Josh|last=Kraushaar|url=http://www.politico.com/news/stories/0510/37790.html|title=Rep. Raul Labrador wins Idaho primary upset|publisher=''[[Politico]]''|date=2010-05-26|accessdate=2010-06-28}}</ref> In the general election, Labrador defeated first-term Democratic incumbent [[Walt Minnick]] 51%-41%.<ref>[http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=488779 Labrador v Minnick], ourcampaigns.com; accessed November 15, 2014.</ref>

;2012
{{See also|United States House of Representatives elections in Idaho, 2012#District 1}}
Labrador was challenged in the Republican primary by Reed McCandless, but defeated him 81%-19%.<ref>[http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=751352 Raúl Labrador v Reed McCandless], ourcampaigns.com; accessed November 15, 2014.</ref> In the general election, Labrador won reelection to a second term, defeating former [[NFL]] [[wide receiver]] [[Jimmy Farris]], 63%-31%.<ref>[http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=705005 Raúl Labrador v Jimmy Farris], ourcampaigns.com; accessed November 15, 2014.</ref>

;2014
{{See also|United States House of Representatives elections in Idaho, 2014#District 1|Idaho gubernatorial election, 2014}}
On August 14, 2013, Labrador decided not to challenge incumbent Idaho Governor [[Butch Otter]] in the Republican primary, instead running for reelection to Congress for a third term.<ref>[http://www.politico.com/story/2013/08/raul-labrador-idaho-95547.html Labrador decides not to challenge Otter for governorship of Idaho], politico.com; accessed November 15, 2014.</ref><ref>{{cite news|url=http://latino.foxnews.com/latino/politics/2013/08/14/rep-raul-labrador-to-seek-re-election-ends-speculation-about-run-for-governor|work=Fox News|title=Rep. Raul Labrador To Seek Re-Election, Ends Speculation About Run For Governor|date=August 14, 2013}}</ref>

On August 19, 2013, Democratic State Representative [[Shirley Ringo]] decided to challenge Labrador instead of running for an eighth term in the Idaho state legislature.<ref>
[http://m.lmtribune.com/blogs/political_theater/article_e38ee948-0913-11e3-852e-0019bb30f31a.html Shirley Ringo challenges Labrador for congressional seat], m.lmtribune.com; accessed November 15, 2014.</ref>

In early June 2014 House majority leader [[Eric Cantor]] of Virginia lost the Republican primary for his Congressional seat, and announced he would step down from his leadership role July 31, 2014. Labrador announced on June 13 that he would challenge House Majority Whip, Representative [[Kevin McCarthy (California politician)|Kevin McCarthy]] for the leadership position. In a vote held June 19, 2014 the House selected McCarthy.<ref name="ReutersML">{{cite news|url=http://www.reuters.com/article/2014/06/13/us-usa-congress-cantor-labrador-idUSKBN0EO21820140613|title=Republican Rep. Labrador running for House majority leader post|last=Cornwell|first=Jane|date=June 13, 2014|publisher=[[Reuters]]|accessdate=June 17, 2014}}</ref>

===Tenure===
[[File:Raul Labrador by Gage Skidmore 2.jpg|thumb|right|Raúl Labrador speaking at an event in Las Vegas, Nevada in October 2011.]]
Days after the election he sharply criticized losing presidential candidate [[Mitt Romney]]'s comments about Hispanics.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.washingtonpost.com/politics/romney-sinks-quickly-in-republicans-esteem/2012/11/16/f1941ed0-2ffe-11e2-a30e-5ca76eeec857_story.html|title=Romney sinks quickly in Republicans’ esteem|date=November 17, 2012|publisher=[[Washington Post]]|author=Dag Eggen}}</ref>

;Immigration
Labrador was a member of the "Group of Eight," a bipartisan group of House members working on immigration reform legislation,<ref>[http://www.bloomberg.com/news/2013-04-05/puerto-rico-born-labrador-top-republican-on-immigration.html Bloomberg article on Labrador's role in immigration reform]</ref> but on June 5, 2013, he left the negotiations because he wanted language in the bill requiring that undocumented immigrants be responsible for their own health care costs.<ref>{{cite web|title=Conservative Labrador quits House immigration group|url=http://thehill.com/homenews/house/303761-rep-labrador-quits-house-immigration-group|publisher=The HIll|accessdate=5 June 2013}}</ref> Labrador said he would use his position on the House Judiciary Committee to pass immigration reform legislation.  

On June 18, 2013, he joined a majority of his Judiciary Committee colleagues in voting for the "SAFE Act" to bolster interior enforcement of immigration laws.<ref>{{cite web|title=LABRADOR VOTES FOR SAFE ACT AT JUDICIARY COMMITTEE MARKUP|url=http://labrador.house.gov/press-releases/labrador-votes-for-safe-act-at-judiciary-committee-markup|publisher=Labrador website|accessdate=18 June 2013}}</ref> On June 19, 2013, he joined a majority of his Judiciary Committee colleagues in voting for the "AG Act" to improve the temporary agricultural guest worker program.<ref>{{cite web|title=LABRADOR VOTES FOR AG ACT AT JUDICIARY COMMITTEE MARKUP|url=http://labrador.house.gov/press-releases/labrador-votes-for-ag-act-at-judiciary-committee-markup|publisher=Labrador website|accessdate=June 19, 2013}}</ref>

On July 6 2014 episode of ''[[Meet the Press]]'' Labrador stated that what the Obama administration needs to do is "immediately deport" young undocumented immigrants. The comment came as part of a discussion about the estimated 52,000 unaccompanied minors from Central America who have tried to cross the border since October.<ref>[http://www.washingtonpost.com/blogs/the-fix/wp/2014/07/07/americans-dont-want-mass-deportations-but-that-are-sort-of-ok-with-increased-deportations "Americans don't want mass deportations but are sort of OK with increased deportations"], washingtonpost.com; accessed November 15, 2014.</ref>

;Health Care
Labrador supports the repeal of the Patient Protection and Affordable Health Care Act because he believes it will raise costs and kill jobs. He believes the best way to give Americans more affordable health insurance is with market-based policies that will decrease healthcare costs. {{cn|date=November 2014}}

;Education
In October 2011, Labrador introduced the American Education Act, intended to address the lack of students entering high-tech fields such as engineering and medical technology by providing incentives to do so. The bill would also allow foreign students in these fields who had job offers from American companies to acquire residency.<ref>[http://votesmart.org/public-statement/645739/labrador-introduces-american-innovation-and-education-act Profile re American Education Act], votesmart.org; accessed November 15, 2104.</ref> While in the Idaho State House of Representatives, Labrador voted for several bills to fund online education within the state of Idaho.<ref>[http://votesmart.org/candidate/key-votes/57391/raul-labrador/27/education Profile re education], votesmart.org; accessed November 15, 2014.</ref>

;Energy
Labrador believes alternative energy sources should be explored, including nuclear, hydroelectric, carbon neutral biomass, wind, solar and geothermal,<ref>[http://votesmart.org/public-statement/631848/issue-position-natural-resources-and-energy Profile re natural resources], votesmart.org; accessed November 15, 2014.</ref> but opposes government subsidies for the development of these resources, saying they will develop on their own.<ref>[http://votesmart.org/public-statement/610084/pompeo-labrador-introduce-new-resolution-targeting-all-energy-subsidies Profile re energy subsidies], votesmart.org; accessed November 15, 2014.</ref> He has made clear that he thinks the government needs to decrease regulation of geothermal energy, claiming Idaho's development of it is being impeded by governmental regulation, and has sponsored legislation to relax the regulations.<ref>[http://votesmart.org/public-statement/626731/house-committee-passes-labrador-geothermal-energy-bill Profile re geothermal energy], votesmart.org; accessed November 15, 2014.</ref>

;Fiscal policy
In July 2012 Labrador supported extending the budget for an extra six months in order to "prevent a crisis."<ref name="Conservatives CR">{{cite news|title=Conservatives Down with a CR|url=http://influencealley.nationaljournal.com/2012/07/conservatives-down-with-a-cr.php?mrefid=site_search|newspaper=National Journal|accessdate=November 15, 2014}}</ref>

In 2010 Labrador signed a pledge sponsored by [[Americans for Prosperity]] promising to vote against any Global Warming legislation that would raise taxes.<ref>[http://americansforprosperity.org/files/Labrador_Raul.pdf Profile of Raúl Labrador], americansforprosperity.org; accessed November 15, 2014.</ref>

;Agriculture
Labrador voted for the farm bill on January 29, 2014.

==Personal life==
Labrador lives in [[Eagle, Idaho]], with his wife, Rebecca, and their five children.<ref>[http://www.labrador4idaho.com/about-Raul-labrador Labrador bio from his campaign website]</ref> He is the first [[The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints|Mormon]] to represent Idaho's [[Idaho's 1st congressional district|1st district]]. {{cn|date=November 2014}}
